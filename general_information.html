General Information about Aircraft Fuel System

Introduction to Aircraft Refueling Systems

An aircraft refueling system is a critical component in aviation, ensuring that aircraft receive the fuel needed for safe and efficient operation. The primary purpose of the refueling system is fuel transfer—delivering the correct type and amount of fuel to the aircraft in a safe and controlled manner.

Types of Refueling Systems

Overwing Refueling

Overwing refueling is often seen in smaller or general aviation aircraft, such as Cessnas. In this method, fuel is pumped directly into the tank openings located on top of the aircraft wings. Overwing refueling is similar to how a car is refueled and usually requires manual intervention.

Underwing (Single Point) Refueling

Underwing refueling is the standard method for most commercial and larger aircraft. This method uses a single-point refueling port beneath the wing, which connects to a refueling truck or hydrant system. Underwing refueling is generally faster and can handle larger volumes of fuel.

Types of Fuel

The type of fuel used depends on the aircraft’s engine type:

  • Jet Fuel (Jet A or Jet A-1): Standard for commercial and turbine-powered aircraft due to its energy efficiency and availability.
  • Avgas (Aviation Gasoline): Used in piston-engine aircraft, commonly found in smaller planes like Cessnas.

Safety Measures During Refueling

Safety is paramount during the refueling process to prevent accidents and ensure smooth operations. Some key safety practices include:

  • Grounding and Bonding:
    • Grounding: Connecting the aircraft and refueling equipment to the ground prevents static electricity buildup, which can cause sparks and ignite fuel vapors.
    • Bonding: Ensures all equipment is at the same electrical potential, preventing static discharge.
  • Spill Prevention: Ground crews are trained to handle fuel hoses properly and monitor fuel flow to prevent spills. Quick reaction to spills helps mitigate potential hazards.
  • Deadman’s Control: This safety feature requires the operator to maintain continuous pressure on the control, ensuring the fuel flow stops if the operator lets go. This prevents fuel overflow and spills if the operator becomes distracted or steps away.
  • Static Electricity Concerns: Static electricity can build up during the refueling process due to fuel movement through hoses. If not managed, static electricity could ignite fuel vapors. Grounding and bonding prevent this hazard.

Refueling System Components

  • Fueling Manifold: A system of pipes and valves that directs fuel flow from the source to the aircraft. It allows for controlled and directed fuel distribution.
  • Pressure Control Valve: This valve regulates the fuel flow rate during refueling, ensuring a steady and safe delivery of fuel. It prevents over-pressurization, which could damage the fuel tanks or cause leaks.

Coordination During Refueling

Communication between the aircraft and ground crew is essential for safe refueling. Hand signals or radio communication allow clear coordination to start, stop, or adjust the refueling process. This communication ensures that all personnel understand their roles and can react quickly to any issues.

Summary of Key Points

  • Purpose: Aircraft refueling systems are used primarily for safe and efficient fuel transfer.
  • Types: Overwing (used in smaller aircraft) and underwing (standard in commercial aircraft).
  • Fuel Types: Jet Fuel (Jet A, Jet A-1) for turbines and Avgas for piston engines.
  • Safety: Grounding, bonding, spill prevention, Deadman’s control, and static electricity precautions are essential.
  • Components: Fueling manifold directs flow, and the pressure control valve regulates fuel rate.
  • Coordination: Communication between ground crew and pilots ensures safe refueling.
